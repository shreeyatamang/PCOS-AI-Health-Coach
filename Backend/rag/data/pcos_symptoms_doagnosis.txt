PCOS OVERVIEW - SYMPTOMS AND DIAGNOSIS

What is PCOS:
Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is a common hormonal disorder affecting approximately 8-13% of women of reproductive age globally. It is the most common cause of anovulation (failure to release eggs) and a leading cause of female infertility. Up to 70% of women with PCOS worldwide are undiagnosed. PCOS persists beyond the reproductive years and is associated with long-term metabolic and cardiovascular risks.

PCOS is caused by higher than normal levels of androgens (male hormones) produced by the ovaries. This causes reproductive hormones to become imbalanced, leading to irregular menstrual periods, abnormal ovulation, and other symptoms.

Diagnosis Criteria (Rotterdam Criteria):
A diagnosis of PCOS requires at least 2 of the following 3 features:
1. Hyperandrogenism: Clinical signs (excess hair, acne) or elevated androgen levels on blood test
2. Ovulatory Dysfunction: Irregular or absent periods (oligo-ovulation or anovulation)
3. Polycystic Ovary Morphology: Multiple follicle cysts visible on ultrasound, OR elevated anti-Mullerian hormone (AMH) levels

Important: Despite the name, you do NOT need to have cysts on your ovaries to be diagnosed with PCOS.

PCOS Phenotypes:
PCOS can be categorized into four phenotypes:
- Phenotype A: Ovulatory dysfunction + Hyperandrogenism + Polycystic ovaries (most severe)
- Phenotype B: Hyperandrogenism + Ovulatory dysfunction
- Phenotype C: Polycystic ovaries + Hyperandrogenism
- Phenotype D: Ovulatory dysfunction + Polycystic ovaries (mildest)

Common Symptoms:

Menstrual:
- Irregular periods (fewer than 8 periods per year, or cycles longer than 35 days)
- Absent periods (amenorrhea)
- Very heavy or prolonged bleeding when periods do occur
- Unpredictable ovulation

Hormonal/Physical:
- Excess facial or body hair (hirsutism) - typically on face, chest, stomach, back
- Acne on face, chest, or back
- Oily skin
- Male-pattern hair thinning or hair loss at the scalp
- Weight gain, especially around the abdomen (PCOS belly)
- Skin darkening in body creases (acanthosis nigricans) - sign of insulin resistance

Metabolic:
- Insulin resistance (present in 60-80% of women with PCOS)
- Fatigue and low energy
- Difficulty losing weight
- Sugar cravings

Reproductive:
- Difficulty conceiving (infertility)
- Higher risk of miscarriage
- Higher risk of pregnancy complications

Lean PCOS:
- Some women have PCOS with a normal BMI - called lean PCOS
- Lean women with PCOS can still have insulin resistance and hormonal imbalances
- Symptoms and treatment are similar regardless of weight

How PCOS is Diagnosed:

Medical History:
- Menstrual cycle history (regularity, frequency, flow)
- Symptoms review (hair growth, acne, weight changes)
- Family history (PCOS and type 2 diabetes increase risk)

Physical Examination:
- Check for signs of excess androgen (hair growth pattern, acne, scalp hair loss)
- Blood pressure measurement
- Check for acanthosis nigricans (skin darkening)

Blood Tests:
- Total and free testosterone levels
- LH (Luteinizing Hormone) and FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone) ratio
- Anti-Mullerian Hormone (AMH) - elevated in PCOS
- Fasting glucose and insulin levels
- HbA1c (to check for diabetes)
- Thyroid function tests (to rule out thyroid disorders)
- Prolactin levels (to rule out other causes)
- Lipid profile (cholesterol)
- Vitamin D levels

Ultrasound:
- Transvaginal or abdominal ultrasound to visualize ovaries
- Polycystic morphology: 20+ follicles per ovary, or enlarged ovary volume
- Not always necessary for diagnosis if other criteria are met
- Not reliable in teenagers (due to normally high follicle counts)

Insulin Resistance Testing:
- Routine insulin resistance testing is NOT currently recommended for standard diagnosis
- However, fasting glucose and oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) can assess metabolic risk

Conditions to Rule Out:
Before diagnosing PCOS, doctors must rule out:
- Thyroid disorders
- High prolactin (hyperprolactinemia)
- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia
- Cushing syndrome
- Androgen-secreting tumors

Long-Term Health Risks of PCOS:
- Type 2 diabetes (significantly increased risk)
- Cardiovascular disease and high blood pressure
- High cholesterol
- Metabolic syndrome
- Obstructive sleep apnea
- Endometrial hyperplasia and endometrial cancer (due to lack of regular periods)
- Depression and anxiety
- Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease
- Pregnancy complications (gestational diabetes, preeclampsia)

Risk Factors:
- Family history of PCOS
- Family history of type 2 diabetes
- Obesity or insulin resistance
- Premature puberty

When to See a Doctor:
- Irregular or absent periods
- Difficulty getting pregnant
- Signs of excess androgens (excess hair, acne, scalp hair loss)
- Rapid weight gain especially around the abdomen
- Signs of insulin resistance (fatigue, sugar cravings, darkened skin in body creases)

Early diagnosis and treatment leads to better long-term outcomes.
