PCOS TREATMENT - COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE

Overview:
There is no cure for PCOS, but symptoms can be effectively managed through lifestyle changes, medications, and targeted treatments. Treatment is personalized based on a woman's symptoms, goals, and whether she wishes to become pregnant.

First-Line Treatment: Lifestyle Modification
Before medications, lifestyle changes are always the first recommendation for PCOS management:
- Healthy, balanced diet (low GI, Mediterranean, or DASH style)
- Regular physical activity (150+ minutes per week)
- Weight management (even 5-10% weight loss significantly improves symptoms)
- Sleep improvement and stress management
- These changes improve insulin sensitivity, regulate hormones, and restore menstrual regularity

Hormonal Treatments:

1. Combined Oral Contraceptive Pills (COCPs / Birth Control Pills)
- First-line pharmacological treatment for menstrual irregularity and hyperandrogenism
- Contains synthetic estrogen and progestin
- Benefits: regulates periods, reduces acne, reduces excess hair growth (hirsutism), lowers androgen levels
- Increases sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), which lowers free testosterone
- Not suitable if pregnancy is the goal or if there are contraindications (blood clot risk, etc.)
- Lower estrogen dose preparations are preferred to minimize side effects
- Regular follow-up with your doctor is important

2. Progestin Therapy
- Taken for 10-14 days every 1-3 months to induce a withdrawal bleed
- Protects the uterine lining from endometrial hyperplasia (thickening)
- Does not reduce androgens or acne like combined pills do
- Option for women who cannot use estrogen-containing contraceptives

Insulin-Sensitizing Medications:

3. Metformin
- Most commonly prescribed insulin-sensitizing drug for PCOS
- Improves insulin sensitivity and lowers insulin levels
- Reduces androgen levels
- Helps regulate menstrual cycles
- Supports weight management (modest effect)
- May improve fertility as a secondary benefit
- Can improve mood by reducing insulin resistance-linked depression
- Recommended primarily for metabolic features of PCOS
- Side effects: nausea, diarrhea (usually resolve with time or food intake)

Anti-Androgen Medications:

4. Spironolactone
- Blocks androgen receptors, reducing effects of male hormones
- Used to treat hirsutism (excess hair growth) and scalp hair loss
- Also reduces acne
- Often used alongside hormonal contraceptives
- Must use contraception during treatment (teratogenic - harmful to fetus)

5. Flutamide and Finasteride
- Alternative anti-androgens used in some cases
- Primarily for scalp hair loss and hirsutism
- Also require contraception during use

Fertility Treatments:

6. Letrozole (Femara)
- Currently the preferred first-line treatment for inducing ovulation in PCOS
- An aromatase inhibitor originally used for breast cancer
- More effective than clomiphene citrate for PCOS-related infertility
- Typically taken on days 3-7 of the menstrual cycle

7. Clomiphene Citrate (Clomid)
- Historically the most used ovulation induction drug
- Stimulates the release of FSH and LH to trigger ovulation
- Now considered second-line after letrozole for PCOS
- Multiple pregnancy risk is slightly elevated

8. Gonadotropins (FSH injections)
- Used if oral medications fail to induce ovulation
- Requires careful monitoring to avoid ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS)
- Administered by injection

9. Metformin + Ovulation Induction
- Combining metformin with clomiphene improves ovulation rates in some women with PCOS

10. In Vitro Fertilization (IVF)
- Used when other fertility treatments are unsuccessful
- Women with PCOS generally have good outcomes with IVF due to high egg numbers
- Risk of ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS) is higher in PCOS - managed carefully

Surgical Option:

11. Laparoscopic Ovarian Drilling (LOD)
- Minor surgical procedure where small holes are made in the ovary with a laser
- Reduces androgen-producing tissue in the ovary
- Helps restore regular ovulation in some women
- Used when medications have failed
- Effect may be temporary

Supplements (Supportive, Not Replacement for Medical Treatment):

Inositol (Myo-Inositol + D-Chiro-Inositol):
- Improves insulin sensitivity and ovulatory function
- Recommended ratio: 40:1 myo-inositol to D-chiro-inositol
- Offers limited clinical benefit compared to metformin but has fewer side effects
- Can be used alongside other treatments

Vitamin D:
- Supplementation recommended if deficient (very common in PCOS)
- Improves insulin sensitivity and supports hormonal regulation
- Vitamin D regulates menstrual cycles and follicular development

Omega-3 Fatty Acids:
- Reduces inflammation, insulin resistance, and androgen levels
- 12 weeks of supplementation shows measurable benefits
- Safe and widely available

Berberine:
- An emerging natural alternative to metformin
- Shows promise in improving insulin sensitivity
- More research is ongoing

Managing Acne in PCOS:
- Combined oral contraceptives reduce acne effectively
- Anti-androgen medications (spironolactone) reduce oil production
- Topical retinoids and antibiotics can be added
- Avoid picking - leads to scarring
- Consult a dermatologist for persistent acne

Managing Hirsutism (Excess Hair):
- Anti-androgens reduce new hair growth over 6-12 months
- Hair removal methods: laser hair removal (most effective long-term), waxing, threading, shaving, electrolysis
- Eflornithine cream slows facial hair regrowth
- Results from anti-androgen medications take time - be patient

Managing Hair Loss (Scalp):
- Anti-androgens (spironolactone, finasteride) can slow progression
- Minoxidil (topical) used for female-pattern hair loss
- Iron deficiency worsens hair loss - check iron levels

Long-Term Health Monitoring:
Women with PCOS should have regular check-ups for:
- Blood pressure
- Fasting glucose and HbA1c (diabetes screening)
- Cholesterol and lipid panel
- Thyroid function
- Mental health screening
- Endometrial health (irregular periods increase endometrial cancer risk)

Important Note:
All treatment decisions should be made with your healthcare provider based on your individual symptoms, goals, and medical history. PCOS management is a partnership between you and your care team.
